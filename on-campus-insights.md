<h2> Insights </h2>

<h3>1. Things I learned about myself</h3>

Reflecting on my teaching internship, the most significant thing I learned about myself is the depth of my pedagogical agility and resilience. I discovered that I thrive not as a mere dispenser of knowledge, but as a guide, naturally using the art of questioning to synthesize student answers and build the bigger picture. Even when my ideal, interactive teaching style wasn't an option, I learned that I have the internal capacity to adapt—such as successfully channeling a high-energy "YouTuber" persona for a pre-recorded Math class. Beyond my instructional methods, stepping up as a Grade 12 research panelist and hosting the family day taught me that my commitment to education extends well beyond my specific subject area. Earning 2nd place for Best Teaching Intern for Science was a validating milestone, but it also taught me a hard lesson about the pressure of success: I now know I must learn to navigate the high expectations of mentors and peers moving forward.

<h3>2. Things I improved about </h3>

Throughout this deployment, the most tangible thing I improved about myself was my ability to leverage technology to combat student screen fatigue. I transformed my drawing tablet into a dynamic instructional tool, mastering how to visually map out concepts in real-time to overcome the usually static nature of online presentations. I also significantly improved my professional footprint and confidence. By actively engaging in the broader school community and leaning into leadership roles, I built a strong foundation for my career. I am incredibly proud of how I have refined my teaching personas and digital delivery, knowing that these improvements have opened up massive opportunities for me to seamlessly blend these modern tech skills with traditional instruction once I finally transition into a physical, face-to-face classroom.

<h3>3. Things I still have to improve </h3>

However, this online setup also heavily exposed the areas I still have to improve on, most notably my reliance on live feedback to evaluate learning. Because my teaching identity is so deeply anchored in real-time interaction and on-the-spot questioning, I struggle significantly to gauge student mastery when that feedback loop is removed, like in asynchronous formats. Developing effective, engaging asynchronous assessments is definitely a weak muscle I need to build. Moving forward, my primary focus is to seize the opportunity to expand my evaluation toolkit. I need to research and practice alternative ways to test understanding so that I can become a more versatile evaluator—one who can ensure students are actually grasping the material, even when faced with the constant threat of "black screens" and digital burnout.
