# Insights

## 1. Things I Learned About Myself
Looking back at my time at CCNHS, the most surprising thing I learned about myself was my profound capacity to connect with my students. I initially thought that being a good teacher meant just delivering the content, but I realized that my love for teaching is deeply rooted in the relationships I build in the classroom. I also learned a crucial lesson about my own psychology: I discovered that I am highly susceptible to performance anxiety. When my mentor and other teachers observed my first class with Grade 10 - Cabahug, my fear of looking incompetent caused me to abandon my favored teaching strategy—the art of questioning—and default to a rushed, teacher-centered lecture.

On a more positive note, I learned that setting strict personal boundaries does not make me a "terror teacher" or alienate me from my class. Paradoxically, by making sure my students knew I was their teacher first and foremost, I created a safe space where we could actually have more fun and enjoy the learning process together. Lastly, I learned that I can be a bit too trusting or naive in unfamiliar situations, like when I easily believed the students in Villanueva who claimed their class started an hour later than scheduled!

## 2. Things I Improved About Myself
Throughout this journey, my adaptability and teaching methodology saw the most significant improvement. I learned how to treat my grueling schedule of six back-to-back classes not just as a burden, but as a live laboratory. By using my earlier classes as a testing ground, I was able to rapidly iterate and refine my lesson plans on the fly, delivering my most optimized and confident teaching to my final class, Rago.

I also drastically improved my classroom management skills by carefully observing and emulating my mentor, Madame Guiroy. Instead of trying to talk over a chaotic room like I did on my first day, I learned the power of silent authority—waiting at the door until the students settled themselves. I also became more creative and assertive in handling disciplinary issues. For instance, instead of just letting chronic tardiness slide in my Villanueva class, I implemented a rule where latecomers had to answer a question or provide a valid reason before they were allowed to sit, which successfully reduced late arrivals over time.

## 3. Things I Still Have to Improve On
While I am proud of my growth, there are definitely areas I need to continue working on. First, I need to improve my initial assertiveness and trust my instincts right from the start. On my very first day, I sat in a chaotic room just waiting for time to pass because I didn't have the presence of mind to take control and ask the students to introduce themselves. I need to be proactive, not reactive, when establishing classroom culture on day one.

Second, I need to continue refining my time management and pacing. While I successfully transitioned back to a student-centered, Socratic method of questioning, I still struggle with balancing that deep engagement with the reality of finishing the required syllabus on time. It is a delicate balancing act that requires more practice.

Finally, I need to improve how I manage my own well-being and physical boundaries. Surviving six back-to-back classes with only a 15-minute break—which I admitted I never even used to eat my lunch—is a recipe for severe burnout. To be the best teacher I can be for my students, I have to learn how to carve out time to rest, recharge, and actually eat during my shift.
